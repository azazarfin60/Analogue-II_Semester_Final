## 1. Internal Architecture and Pin Configuration
*[Appeared in: 2024 Q7(a), 2022 Q4(a), 2019 Q7(a), 2018 Q7(a)]*

### 1.1 Pin Roles
*   **Pin 1 (GND):** Ground reference.
*   **Pin 2 (TRIG):** Trigger input. When this voltage drops below $1/3 V_{CC}$, the output (Pin 3) is forced HIGH.
*   **Pin 3 (OUT):** Output terminal (sinks/sources up to $200\text{ mA}$).
*   **Pin 4 (RST):** Reset input (active LOW). Ties to $V_{CC}$ if unused.
*   **Pin 5 (CV):** Control Voltage. Accesses the internal $2/3 V_{CC}$ divider node to modulate threshold voltage levels.
*   **Pin 6 (THR):** Threshold input. When this voltage rises above $2/3 V_{CC}$, the output is forced LOW and the discharge transistor turns ON.
*   **Pin 7 (DIS):** Discharge pin. Connected to the collector of an internal NPN transistor. Grounded when the output is LOW.
*   **Pin 8 (VCC):** Positive supply rail ($+4.5\text{ V}$ to $+15\text{ V}$).

### 1.2 Internal Block Description
*   **Voltage Divider:** Three matched $5\text{ k}\Omega$ resistors connected in series from $V_{CC}$ to Ground, setting internal reference potentials at $2/3 V_{CC}$ and $1/3 V_{CC}$.
*   **Comparators:**
    *   **Threshold Comparator:** Compares Pin 6 (Threshold) to the $2/3 V_{CC}$ node.
    *   **Trigger Comparator:** Compares Pin 2 (Trigger) to the $1/3 V_{CC}$ node.
*   **Flip-Flop:** An RS Flip-Flop driven by the comparator outputs, storing the digital state.
*   **Discharge Transistor:** An NPN transistor whose base is driven by the inverted output of the Flip-Flop. It turns ON when Pin 3 goes LOW.

## 2. Astable Multivibrator Mode

### 2.1 Waveform Mechanics and Timing Cycle
*[Appeared in: 2022 Q4(b), 2020 Q7(a)]*

In Astable mode, the 555 timer operates as a free-running oscillator:

```
                      +Vcc
                       |
                      [ ] RA
                       |
                       +------- Pin 7 (Discharge)
                       |
                      [ ] RB
                       |
                       +------- Pin 6 (Threshold) / Pin 2 (Trigger)
                       |
                     +---+ C
                     | | |
                     +---+
                       |
                      Gnd
```

1.  **Charging Phase ($t_H$):** Emitter capacitor $C$ charges from $1/3 V_{CC}$ toward $V_{CC}$ through resistors $R_A$ and $R_B$. During this time, the output is HIGH ($V_{CC}$) and the discharge BJT is OFF.
    $$
    t_H = 0.693 (R_A + R_B) C
    $$
2.  **Discharging Phase ($t_L$):** When the capacitor voltage reaches the $2/3 V_{CC}$ threshold, the upper comparator resets the flip-flop. The output goes LOW ($0\text{V}$) and the discharge transistor turns ON. The capacitor discharges from $2/3 V_{CC}$ down to $1/3 V_{CC}$ through resistor $R_B$ directly into Pin 7.
    $$
    t_L = 0.693 R_B C
    $$
3.  **Total Period & Frequency:**
    $$
    T = t_H + t_L = 0.693 (R_A + 2 R_B) C \Rightarrow f_o = \frac{1.44}{(R_A + 2 R_B) C}
    $$
4.  **Duty Cycle ($D$):**
    $$
    D = \frac{t_H}{T} = \frac{R_A + R_B}{R_A + 2 R_B} \times 100\%
    $$
Since $R_A > 0$, the duty cycle is always strictly greater than $50\%$.

### 2.2 Worked Astable Design
*[Appeared in: 2019 Q7(c)]*

**Problem Details:**
Design a 555 astable oscillator with an output frequency $f_o = 3.5\text{ kHz}$ and a duty cycle $D = 60\%$.

#### Step-by-Step Solution:

##### Step 1: Solve for the resistor relationship
$$
D = \frac{R_A + R_B}{R_A + 2 R_B} = 0.60 \Rightarrow R_A + R_B = 0.6 R_A + 1.2 R_B
$$
$$
0.4 R_A = 0.2 R_B \Rightarrow R_B = 2 R_A
$$

##### Step 2: Solve for RC components
$$
f_o = \frac{1.44}{(R_A + 2 R_B) C} = 3500\text{ Hz}
$$
Substitute $R_B = 2 R_A$:
$$
3500 = \frac{1.44}{(R_A + 4 R_A) C} = \frac{1.44}{5 R_A C} \Rightarrow 5 R_A C = 4.114 \times 10^{-4}
$$
Choose standard capacitor value $C = 10\text{ nF} = 10^{-8}\text{ F}$:
$$
5 R_A (10^{-8}) = 4.114 \times 10^{-4} \Rightarrow R_A = \frac{4.114 \times 10^{-4}}{5 \times 10^{-8}} \approx 8228\ \Omega
$$
Use standard resistor values:
*   $R_A = 8.2\text{ k}\Omega$
*   $R_B = 2 R_A \approx 16.5\text{ k}\Omega$

## 3. Astable Mode with Bypass Diode
*[Appeared in: 2018 Q7(b)]*

### 3.1 Timing Alterations with Ideal Diode
To achieve a duty cycle of $50\%$ or less, a diode is placed in parallel across resistor $R_B$ (anode at Pin 7, cathode at Pin 6/2):

```
                        RA
        Pin 7 o--------[  ]--------+----o +Vcc
              |                    |
             --- D1 (Bypass)      [ ] RB
             \ /                   |
              |                    |
        Pin 6 o--------------------+----o C (to Gnd)
```

1.  **Charging Phase ($t_H$):** Current flows from $V_{CC}$ through $R_A$. The forward-biased diode $D_1$ bypasses $R_B$, providing a low-resistance path to the capacitor. The capacitor charges *only* through $R_A$:
    $$
    t_H = 0.693 R_A C
    $$
2.  **Discharging Phase ($t_L$):** Current discharges into Pin 7. The diode is reverse-biased (blocking). The capacitor must discharge through $R_B$:
    $$
    t_L = 0.693 R_B C
    $$
3.  **Duty Cycle ($D$):**
    $$
    D = \frac{t_H}{t_H + t_L} = \frac{R_A}{R_A + R_B} \times 100\%
    $$
Setting $R_A < R_B$ enables duty cycles below $50\%$ (e.g., $R_A = R_B$ yields a perfect $50\%$ square wave).

---

### 3.2 Worked Bypass Diode Numerical
*[Appeared in: 2018 Q7(b)]*

**Problem Details:**
Calculate the duty cycle of a 555 astable circuit containing an ideal bypass diode across $R_B$. $R_A = 1\text{ k}\Omega, R_B = 10\text{ k}\Omega$.

#### Step-by-Step Solution:
$$
D = \frac{R_A}{R_A + R_B} \times 100\% = \frac{1\text{ k}\Omega}{1\text{ k}\Omega + 10\text{ k}\Omega} \times 100\% = \frac{1}{11} \times 100\% \approx 9.09\%
$$

## 4. Monostable Multivibrator Mode

### 4.1 Timing capacitor charge equation & Pulse Width Proof
*[Appeared in: 2020 Q6(a)]*

In a monostable (one-shot) configuration, the timer rests in its stable state (output LOW) until triggered by a negative pulse at Pin 2. Once triggered, the discharge transistor shuts OFF and the output goes HIGH. Capacitor $C$ charges from $0\text{V}$ toward $V_{CC}$ through a single resistor $R$.

```
                      +Vcc
                       |
                      [ ] R
                       |
                       +------- Pin 6 (Threshold) / Pin 7 (Discharge)
                       |
                     +---+ C
                     | | |
                     +---+
                       |
                      Gnd
```

**Proof of Pulse Width $W \approx 1.1 RC$:**
The voltage across a charging capacitor over time is:
$$
v_c(t) = V_{CC} (1 - e^{-t / RC})
$$
The timing cycle ends and the output returns to LOW when the capacitor voltage reaches the upper threshold voltage:
$$
v_c(W) = \frac{2}{3} V_{CC}
$$
Substitute this threshold value into the charging equation:
$$
\frac{2}{3} V_{CC} = V_{CC} (1 - e^{-W / RC}) \Rightarrow \frac{2}{3} = 1 - e^{-W / RC}
$$
$$
e^{-W / RC} = 1 - \frac{2}{3} = \frac{1}{3}
$$
Take the natural logarithm of both sides:
$$
-\frac{W}{RC} = \ln \left( \frac{1}{3} \right) = \ln(1) - \ln(3) = 0 - 1.0986 = -1.0986
$$
$$
W = 1.0986 RC \approx 1.1 RC
$$

### 4.2 Worked Monostable Trigger Waveform Sketch
*[Appeared in: 2018 Q7(c)]*

**Problem Details:**
Sketch the output voltage waveform ($V_o$) of a 555 monostable timer with $R = 4.7\text{ k}\Omega$ and $C = 0.1\ \mu\text{F}$, if it is triggered by negative pulses at $t = 1\text{ ms}$ and $t = 5\text{ ms}$.

#### Step-by-Step Solution:

##### Step 1: Calculate the output pulse width ($W$)
$$
W = 1.1 R C = 1.1 \times (4.7 \times 10^3\ \Omega) \times (0.1 \times 10^{-6}\text{ F}) \approx 0.517\text{ ms}
$$

##### Step 2: Sketch timing sequence
*   **$t = 0$ to $1\text{ ms}$:** Output rests at LOW ($0\text{V}$). Capacitor remains fully discharged ($0\text{V}$).
*   **$t = 1\text{ ms}$:** Trigger arrives. Output instantly transitions HIGH ($V_{CC} \approx 10\text{V}$). Emitter capacitor begins exponentially charging toward $V_{CC}$ through $R$.
*   **$t = 1.517\text{ ms}$:** Emitter capacitor reaches $2/3 V_{CC}$. The comparator resets the flip-flop. Emitter capacitor is instantly discharged to $0\text{V}$ through Pin 7, and the output transitions back to LOW ($0\text{V}$).
*   **$t = 1.517\text{ ms}$ to $5\text{ ms}$:** Output rests at LOW.
*   **$t = 5\text{ ms}$:** Second trigger arrives. Output instantly transitions HIGH again.
*   **$t = 5.517\text{ ms}$:** Capacitor reaches $2/3 V_{CC}$. Output returns to LOW.

```
       Vo (V)
      10+         +-----+                 +-----+
        |         |     |                 |     |
       0+---------+     +-----------------+     +---------
        0         1    1.517              5    5.517     t (ms)
```

## 5. Monostable Applications

### 5.1 Monostable as a Frequency Divider
*[Appeared in: 2021 Q7(b), 2019 Q7(b), CT5 Q1]*

A monostable circuit can function as a **Frequency Divider** by setting its output pulse width $W$ to ignore alternate input trigger pulses:
1.  A continuous negative pulse train with period $T_{in}$ is applied to the Trigger (Pin 2).
2.  The monostable RC time constant is designed such that the output pulse width $W$ is greater than the input period but less than twice the period:
    $$
    T_{in} < W < 2 T_{in}
    $$
3.  **Process:**
    *   The first trigger pulse at $t=0$ initiates the monostable cycle. The output goes HIGH and remains HIGH for the duration $W$.
    *   The second trigger pulse arrives at $t = T_{in}$. Because $T_{in} < W$, the timer is still in its active HIGH state and cannot be retriggered. The capacitor has not reached $2/3 V_{CC}$ yet. Therefore, this second trigger is completely ignored.
    *   The active pulse ends at $t = W$. The capacitor discharges, and the output returns to LOW.
    *   The third trigger pulse arrives at $t = 2 T_{in}$. Because the timer is now in its stable LOW state, this pulse successfully retriggers the cycle.
This process skips every alternating input pulse, generating one output pulse for every two input pulses. The output frequency is exactly halved: $f_{out} = f_{in} / 2$.

### 5.2 Missing Pulse Detector
*[Appeared in: 2020 Q6(c)]*

A circuit designed to output a warning signal if a periodic pulse train misses a cycle.
*   **Configuration:** A standard monostable layout. A PNP transistor is connected directly in parallel across the timing capacitor $C$ (emitter to Pin 6/7, collector to Ground). The base of the PNP transistor and the Trigger input (Pin 2) are tied together and driven by the incoming pulse train.
*   **Operation:**
    1.  The monostable pulse width is set to be slightly longer than the incoming pulse period ($W > T_{in}$).
    2.  Under normal operation, each incoming pulse drops negative, triggering the timer and momentarily turning ON the PNP transistor. The transistor short-circuits the capacitor to ground, resetting its charging cycle to $0\text{V}$.
    3.  Because the reset occurs every period $T_{in}$ (before the capacitor can charge to $2/3 V_{CC}$), the output (Pin 3) remains constantly HIGH.
    4.  If a pulse goes missing, the PNP transistor remains OFF. The capacitor continues charging through $R$ uninterrupted.
    5.  The capacitor voltage hits the $2/3 V_{CC}$ threshold. The timer resets, and the output drops LOW, signaling a missing pulse.

### 5.3 FSK Modulator
*[Appeared in: 2019 Q8(a)]*

Frequency Shift Keying (FSK) shifts the output carrier frequency between two distinct values based on a digital binary input (Logic 0 or Logic 1).
*   **Configuration:** A 555 timer is wired in standard Astable mode. The digital binary data stream is applied directly to **Pin 5 (Control Voltage)**.
*   **Operation:**
    1.  Pin 5 is internally tied to the $2/3 V_{CC}$ node of the voltage divider.
    2.  **Logic HIGH Input:** Applying a positive voltage to Pin 5 increases the upper reference threshold above $2/3 V_{CC}$. The capacitor must charge to a higher voltage, increasing the charge time $t_H$ and resulting in a **lower** output carrier frequency.
    3.  **Logic LOW Input:** Applying $0\text{V}$ (or a lower voltage) drops the reference threshold below $2/3 V_{CC}$. The capacitor charges to a lower voltage and reaches the threshold faster, decreasing $t_H$ and resulting in a **higher** output carrier frequency.
The output toggles frequencies in sync with the digital input stream.
